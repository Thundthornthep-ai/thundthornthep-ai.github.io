Cross-Border Business in Thailand — Legal Guide

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Whether you are a Thai company expanding abroad or a foreign firm entering the Thai market, understanding cross-border investment regulations is essential. Ignorance of the rules can make your business illegal from day one.

The Foreign Business Act B.E. 2542 (1999)

Thailand's primary law governing foreign investment classifies restricted businesses into three lists:

ListRestrictionExamples
List 1Absolutely prohibited for foreignersNewspapers, radio, TV broadcasting, farming, land trading
List 2Permitted with Cabinet approvalWeapons, domestic transportation, antique trading
List 3Permitted with Director-General's licenseRetail, wholesale, accounting, legal, architecture, engineering, construction

"Foreigner" under this Act includes juristic persons where foreign shareholders hold 50% or more of shares, or where foreign partners contribute half or more of capital.

Three Pathways for Foreign Businesses

1. BOI (Board of Investment)

Under the Investment Promotion Act B.E. 2520 (1977), BOI grants privileges to promoted activities: corporate income tax exemption up to 8 years, import duty exemptions on machinery and raw materials, and permission for 100% foreign ownership even in businesses listed under the Foreign Business Act.

2. Treaty of Amity (US-Thailand)

The Treaty of Amity and Economic Relations between Thailand and the United States (1966) grants American companies and citizens the right to hold 100% ownership in most businesses, except communications, transportation, natural resources, banking, and land trading.

3. Free Trade Agreements

Thailand has FTAs with numerous countries and blocs — ASEAN, China, Japan, and Australia — which may provide additional market access benefits and tariff reductions.

Considerations for Thai Companies Expanding Abroad

  • Corporate structure — Branch office, representative office, or subsidiary — each has different tax and liability implications
  • International contracts — Clearly specify governing law and dispute resolution mechanisms
  • Double tax agreements — Thailand has DTAs with over 60 countries to prevent double taxation
  • Foreign exchange controls — Cross-border fund transfers are regulated under the Exchange Control Act B.E. 2485
Practical Tip: Before expanding across borders, thoroughly research the target country's laws, structure your tax position optimally, and engage local counsel as advisors.

Conclusion

Cross-border business opens enormous opportunities but comes with legal complexity. Understanding the regulatory framework from the start helps avoid pitfalls and maximize available benefits.
