Joint Venture — Protecting Your Interests

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Fast-growing businesses often need partners — whether Thai companies combining strengths or foreign firms entering the Thai market. A Joint Venture (JV) is the most common form of business collaboration, but without proper structure from the outset, it can become a trap that is harder to exit than to enter.

Two Types of Joint Venture

1. Contractual JV

No new company is formed. Parties enter into a contract to collaborate on a specific project. This has the characteristics of a partnership under CCC Section 1012. Each party remains a separate legal entity. Ideal for time-limited projects such as construction or real estate development.

2. Corporate JV

A new limited company is formed under CCC Section 1096, with each party holding shares in agreed proportions. Better suited for long-term ventures requiring continuity and a clear governance structure.

Foreign Investment Considerations

The Foreign Business Act B.E. 2542 (1999) classifies businesses into three lists restricting foreign participation. For businesses on List 3, Thai shareholders must hold at least 51% of shares, unless the foreign party obtains a Foreign Business License or qualifies for exemptions through BOI or the Treaty of Amity.

Three Key Principles for JV Agreements

1. Balance of Power

Define board composition, signing authority, and matters requiring unanimous consent. Even a 49% shareholder can have veto power over Reserved Matters requiring a 75% supermajority under CCC Section 1194.

2. Deadlock Resolution

When shareholders cannot agree, the JV needs clear mechanisms: escalation to senior management, mediation, put/call options, or a shotgun clause.

3. Exit Mechanism

Plan the exit from day one: put options, call options, conditions for dissolving the JV, and liquidation procedures under CCC Section 1236.

Practical Tip: A well-structured JV needs three things: (1) a clear, unambiguous agreement, (2) workable deadlock resolution mechanisms, and (3) fair exit provisions for all parties. Most JV failures stem from not addressing these at the outset.

Conclusion

Joint ventures unlock enormous business potential, but they require robust legal foundations. A good JV agreement goes beyond share allocation — it designs decision-making systems, problem-solving mechanisms, and exit paths that all parties can accept.
