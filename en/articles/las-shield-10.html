Litigation vs. Arbitration in Thailand

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

When a business dispute arises, you have a choice — but that choice must be made when the contract is drafted, not when the problem occurs. Choosing between court litigation and arbitration significantly affects costs, timeline, confidentiality, and the final outcome.

Arbitration Under Thai Law

The Arbitration Act B.E. 2545 is the primary law governing arbitration in Thailand. It requires arbitration agreements to be in writing. Parties who have agreed to arbitration cannot file lawsuits in court; the court will dismiss the case upon request by the other party.

THAC — Thailand Arbitration Center

The Thailand Arbitration Center (THAC), established under the Thailand Arbitration Center Act B.E. 2550, is the national arbitration institution. It offers modern arbitration rules including Fast Track procedures for lower-value disputes.

When to Choose Each

Choose Arbitration when:
  • Business confidentiality is paramount
  • The dispute involves specialized technical matters requiring expert adjudicators
  • The counterparty is a foreign entity (easier cross-border enforcement)
  • Speed is a priority
Choose Litigation when:
  • You need full appeal rights
  • The dispute involves matters of public order
  • You need the court's power for interim protective measures
  • The dispute value is low (court fees are cheaper)

Conclusion

Choosing a dispute resolution method is a strategic decision that must be made at the contract drafting stage, not after a problem arises. Both arbitration and litigation have advantages and disadvantages. Selecting the appropriate method for your business and counterparty relationship reduces the cost and time of resolving disputes.
