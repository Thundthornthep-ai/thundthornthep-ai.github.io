Statutes of Limitations in Thai Law — Time the Law Gives You

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Every legal right has an expiry date. If you fail to file a claim within the statutory period, your right becomes time-barred — and the debtor can lawfully refuse to pay.

General Limitation Period

Under Section 193/9 of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC), the general limitation period for claims is 10 years from the date the right became enforceable, unless the law provides otherwise.

How to Count the Period

Under CCC Section 193/12, the limitation period begins on the date the right becomes enforceable. The first day is not counted (Section 193/3, paragraph two). For example, if a loan is due on January 1, the limitation period starts on January 2.

Interruption of Prescription

Under CCC Section 193/14, the limitation period is interrupted when:

  • (1) The debtor acknowledges the debt — through partial payment, interest payment, provision of security, or a written acknowledgment
  • (2) The creditor files a lawsuit
  • (3) The creditor files a petition — such as a claim in bankruptcy proceedings

Upon interruption, the elapsed period is disregarded and a new period begins from the end of the interrupting event (CCC Section 193/15).

Key Point: An expired limitation period does not extinguish the claim itself — it merely gives the debtor a defense. If the debtor fails to raise the defense, the court will not invoke it on its own (CCC Section 193/29).

Conclusion

Limitation periods are one of the most commonly overlooked issues in business disputes. Companies lose enforceable claims simply because they waited too long. Maintaining organized contract records and tracking key deadlines is essential for every business.
