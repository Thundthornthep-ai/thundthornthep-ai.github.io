Unfair Contract Terms — 5 Examples from Thai Court Decisions

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Have you ever signed a contract stating "either party may terminate immediately" or imposing a "30% penalty of total contract value"? Such terms may not be fully enforceable — Thai law protects the disadvantaged party.

Legal Framework

Section 4, paragraph one of the Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 (1997) provides that a term in a contract between a consumer and a business operator, or in a standard form contract, that gives one party an unreasonable advantage over the other shall be enforceable only to the extent that is fair and reasonable.

The law does not make the term void outright — rather, courts have the power to "adjust" it to what is fair.

Scope of Application

Section 4, paragraph three of the Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 provides examples of potentially unfair terms:

  • Terms excluding or limiting liability for breach of contract
  • Terms granting a right to terminate without reasonable grounds
  • Terms granting a right not to perform any contractual obligation
  • Terms fixing unreasonably high damages
  • Other terms of similar nature

Factors Courts Consider

Section 4, paragraph two of the Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 directs courts to consider:

  • Inequality of bargaining power
  • The inducement for entering the contract
  • The economic necessity of the parties
  • Available alternatives at the time of contracting
  • Whether the contract is a standard form (adhesion) contract
For Business Owners: Including unfair terms does not actually protect you — courts will reduce them. It is far better to draft balanced terms from the outset. Balanced contracts withstand judicial scrutiny and build sustainable business relationships.

Conclusion

The Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 serves as a shield for the weaker party. Terms that grant unreasonable advantage are enforceable only to the extent that is fair. Business owners should draft balanced contracts from the start, rather than risk judicial adjustment later.
