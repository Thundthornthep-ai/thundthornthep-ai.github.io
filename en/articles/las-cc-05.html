Limitation of Liability Clauses

The clause that determines "how much damage, how much payment" — the most heavily negotiated provision

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

Why Limit Liability?

In every business contract, the most critical question is: "If something goes wrong, who pays how much?" The Limitation of Liability clause is the tool parties use to cap their financial exposure, ensuring they do not face unlimited liability for breaches.

There are two primary forms: a Cap (maximum aggregate liability, such as "not exceeding the contract value") and an Exclusion (certain types of damages for which no liability arises, such as consequential damages or lost profits).

"A contract without a liability cap is like driving without insurance — the risk has no boundary."

Thai Law Restrictions

CCC Section 373: "An agreement made in advance not to be liable for fraud or gross negligence is void." This means that regardless of what the contract says, liability for intentional misconduct or gross negligence cannot be excluded.

Consequential vs Direct Damages

TypeMeaningExample
Direct DamagesLoss directly caused by the breachCost to repair defective goods
Consequential DamagesDownstream loss resulting from the breachLost profits due to defective goods

Under CCC Section 222, recoverable damages must be losses that "ordinarily arise from non-performance" or losses that "could have been foreseen." This covers both direct and foreseeable consequential damages.

Negotiation Techniques

For the Party Seeking to Limit Liability

  • Propose a Cap equal to the contract value or 12 months of fees
  • Exclude consequential damages, lost profits, and punitive damages
  • Set a Claim Period — e.g., claims must be made within 12 months of the event

For the Party Seeking Protection

  • Negotiate Carve-Outs for: IP infringement, confidentiality breaches, and fraud
  • Ensure the Cap does not apply to indemnification obligations
  • Watch for "Super Caps" — higher caps for fundamental R&W breaches
