Ordinary vs. Special Resolutions in Thai Company Law

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

In a shareholders' meeting, "approved" does not always mean the same thing — some matters require a simple majority, while others demand a three-fourths supermajority. Misunderstanding this distinction can render a resolution voidable.

Ordinary Resolution

Under Section 1194, paragraph one of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC), an ordinary resolution requires a majority vote — more than half of the total votes of shareholders present and entitled to vote. It applies to routine matters such as:

  • Approval of annual financial statements
  • Appointment or removal of directors
  • Appointment of auditors
  • Declaration of dividends
  • Determination of directors' remuneration

Special Resolution

Under Section 1194, paragraph two of the CCC, a special resolution requires not less than three-fourths of the total votes of shareholders present and entitled to vote. It applies to fundamental matters affecting the company's structure.

Practical Considerations

1. Quorum Requirements

Under CCC Section 1178, shareholders holding not less than one-fourth of the company's total shares must be present to constitute a quorum. Without a quorum, any resolution passed is invalid.

2. Notice of Meeting

Notice must be sent at least 7 days in advance, clearly specifying any agenda items requiring a special resolution (CCC Section 1175).

3. Minority Shareholder Protection

Because a special resolution requires 75%, a shareholder holding more than 25% has effective "blocking power" over fundamental changes. This mechanism protects minority shareholders from being overridden on critical decisions.

Tip: Before calling a meeting, verify the resolution type required for each agenda item. Using the wrong type may expose the resolution to challenge under CCC Section 1195.

Conclusion

Ordinary and special resolutions serve as checks and balances within a company. Structural changes demand broader consensus, ensuring that a clear supermajority of shareholders genuinely supports the decision. Understanding this distinction is essential for lawful corporate governance.
