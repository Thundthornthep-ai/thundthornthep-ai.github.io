Governing Law & Dispute Resolution

The clause that determines your fate when disputes arise — court or arbitration?

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

What Is Governing Law?

The Governing Law clause determines which country's law applies to interpret and enforce the contract. In cross-border transactions, this is critical because different legal systems produce different outcomes for the same factual scenario.

Thailand's Act on Conflict of Laws B.E. 2481 (1938) governs which country's law applies when there is an international element. For contracts made in Thailand between Thai parties, choosing a foreign governing law may not be effective to the extent it conflicts with Thai mandatory law.

"Governing law is not just boilerplate — choosing the wrong law can change the outcome of an entire dispute."

Arbitration vs Court

CriterionCourt (Litigation)Arbitration
ConfidentialityPublic — judgments are on recordConfidential — proceedings not disclosed
AppealMultiple levels availableVery limited — nearly impossible to appeal
DurationMay take several yearsTypically 12-18 months
CostCourt fees per statuteArbitrator fees + institutional fees
Cross-Border EnforcementDifficult — no universal treatyEasier — New York Convention

Popular Arbitration Institutions

  • THAC (Thai Arbitration Center) — cost-effective, suitable for domestic disputes
  • SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre) — popular in ASEAN, modern rules
  • ICC (International Chamber of Commerce) — global institution, ideal for large transactions

Practical Tips

Choosing Governing Law

  • Domestic contracts: always use Thai law
  • Cross-border contracts: choose the law you are most familiar with, or the law where assets are located
  • Avoid choosing the law of a country with no connection to the contract — courts may refuse to honor it

Caution

  • Arbitration is ideal for disputes requiring speed and confidentiality, but costs more than court for small claims
  • Poorly drafted arbitration clauses (Pathological Clauses) may prevent the process from starting at all
  • Consider Multi-Tier Clauses: negotiation → mediation → arbitration, to allow amicable resolution first
