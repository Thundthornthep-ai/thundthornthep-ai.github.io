Employment vs. Service Contracts in Thai Law — 7 Key Differences

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

You hire someone to get a job done — but have you ever asked yourself whether the contract you are using is an "employment contract" or a "service contract"? This distinction is not merely semantic. It determines tax obligations, social security contributions, and termination rights.

Legal Foundation

An employment contract (Hire of Services) under Section 575 of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) is a contract whereby the employee agrees to render services to the employer, who agrees to pay wages throughout the period of service. The defining element is the employer's "power of command and control" over how work is performed.

A service contract (Hire of Work) under Section 587 of the CCC is a contract whereby the contractor agrees to accomplish a definite piece of work for the employer, who agrees to pay remuneration for the result. The defining element is "completion of work" — the hirer does not control the method.

Practical Warnings for SMEs

1. Courts Look at Substance, Not Labels

Even if a contract is titled "Service Agreement," courts will reclassify it as employment if, in practice, the hirer controls working hours, directs work methods, and pays monthly wages. Reclassification triggers retroactive severance pay obligations under the Labour Protection Act.

2. Tax Exposure

If the Revenue Department determines that a service contract is actually disguised employment, the company faces back-assessed taxes, penalties, surcharges, and unpaid social security contributions.

3. The 4-Point Anti-Reclassification Check

  • Does the contractor have autonomy over work methods?
  • Can the contractor simultaneously serve other clients?
  • Does the contractor provide their own tools and equipment?
  • Is compensation tied to deliverables rather than time?
Tip: If more than two answers are "No," there is a high risk the arrangement will be reclassified as employment. Consult a lawyer to restructure the contract to match the actual working relationship.

Conclusion

Choosing the right contract type is not just a legal formality — it is a business strategy decision. Service contracts offer flexibility and cost savings, but only if the actual working arrangement genuinely matches. Otherwise, the full weight of employment law obligations falls squarely on the business owner.
