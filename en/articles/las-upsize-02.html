Why Every Partnership Needs a Shareholders' Agreement

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

You started a company with a friend. Everything was great — until money came in and opinions diverged. This is exactly why every business with more than one shareholder needs a Shareholders' Agreement (SHA).

SHA vs Articles of Association

Articles of Association are filed with the Department of Business Development, are publicly accessible, and contain standard provisions as required by law.

A Shareholders' Agreement (SHA) is a private contract between shareholders. It is not registered and can contain far more detailed and flexible provisions — such as board appointment rights, veto rights, share transfer restrictions, and mechanisms for resolving shareholder disputes.

10 Essential Clauses in an SHA

#ClauseWhy It Matters
1Board CompositionDetermines the ratio of directors from each side. Under CCC Section 1167, directors manage the company's affairs.
2Reserved MattersCritical decisions requiring all parties' consent: capital increases, amendments, mergers.
3ROFR (Right of First Refusal)Existing shareholders get first right to purchase shares before outsiders. Per CCC Section 1129 on share transfers.
4Tag-AlongMinority shareholders can sell alongside majority shareholders on the same terms.
5Drag-AlongMajority shareholders can compel minority to sell, ensuring a clean exit transaction.
6Deadlock ResolutionMechanisms when votes are tied: mediation, put/call options, shotgun clause.
7Non-CompetePrevents shareholders from operating competing businesses during the partnership.
8Dividend PolicyPre-agreed criteria and ratios for profit distribution.
9Anti-DilutionProtects shareholding percentages during capital increases.
10Exit MechanismDefines how shareholders can leave: put option, IPO, trade sale.

When Do You Need an SHA?

  • Forming a company with any co-founder — draft it from day one
  • Accepting investors — most investors require an SHA as a condition
  • Entering a joint venture with a foreign partner
  • When shareholding is closely split (50:50 or 60:40) — high deadlock risk
Practical Tip: The best time to negotiate an SHA is while the relationship is strong. Negotiating terms when everyone is aligned produces far more balanced and fair agreements than waiting until disputes arise.

Conclusion

An SHA is not a sign of distrust — it is a tool for clarity. It reduces the chance of conflict and provides mechanisms when problems arise. Sustainable businesses are those with solid legal structures from the start.
