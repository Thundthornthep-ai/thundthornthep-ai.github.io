Due Diligence 101 — What to Check Before Investing

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Whether you are acquiring a business, entering a joint venture, or investing in a company, the first step is always Due Diligence (DD) — a comprehensive investigation before committing capital. A business that looks attractive on the surface may harbor hidden problems. DD is the tool that reveals the truth before you pay.

Four Types of Due Diligence

TypeWhat It ExaminesLed By
Legal DDMaterial contracts, pending litigation, licenses, IP, land titleLawyers
Financial DDFinancial statements, cash flow, liabilities, actual revenueAccountants / Auditors
Tax DDOutstanding tax liabilities, reassessment risk, tax structureTax advisors
Operational DDSystems, key personnel, major client contracts, supply chainBusiness consultants

Legal DD — Key Checkpoints

1. Corporate Structure and Shareholders

Review the company certificate, shareholder register, director list, and articles of association from the Department of Business Development. Check for share disputes, encumbrances on shares, and transfer restrictions.

2. Material Contracts

Review all binding agreements: long-term leases, loan agreements, executive employment contracts, and key client contracts. Look for Change of Control clauses — many contracts allow the counterparty to terminate upon a change in shareholding.

3. Litigation and Disputes

Check pending court cases, government disputes, complaints, and historical litigation.

4. Licenses and Compliance

Verify business licenses, industry-specific permits (e.g., factory licenses), EIA/EHIA approvals, and compliance with the Personal Data Protection Act B.E. 2562 (PDPA).

Red Flags to Watch

  • Hidden liabilities — Off-balance-sheet obligations such as guarantees to third parties
  • Pending litigation — Especially labor, tax, and environmental cases with high potential impact
  • Disadvantageous contracts — Long-term agreements at below-market rates or with restrictive covenants
  • Unclear IP ownership — Unregistered trademarks, software without proper licenses
  • Key employees without non-compete agreements — Risk of talent departing to competitors
  • Expired or incomplete licenses — May require business suspension until renewal
Practical Tip: Effective DD requires a comprehensive checklist, a multi-disciplinary team, and adequate time. Never rush due to seller pressure. Saving on DD costs but discovering problems post-acquisition is far more expensive.

Conclusion

Due diligence is not a mere formality — it is an investment protection tool. DD findings form the basis for price negotiation, contract conditions, and the ultimate decision to proceed or walk away.
