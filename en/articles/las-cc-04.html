Representations & Warranties — The Trust Framework of Contracts

R&W are not just promises — they are the contractual basis for claims when reality does not match expectations

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

What Are Representations & Warranties?

Representations are statements of fact made by one party to induce the other to enter into the contract. Warranties are promises that certain facts are and will remain true. Together, they form the "trust framework" upon which a deal is built. If an R&W proves false, the injured party has a contractual basis to claim damages or, in severe cases, terminate the contract.

While the distinction between "representation" and "warranty" carries significant legal consequences in common law jurisdictions, Thai law under the Civil and Commercial Code does not draw such a sharp line. Nevertheless, Thai business contracts routinely adopt this framework from international practice.

CCC Section 466: The seller warrants that the property sold is free from any defect that would destroy or materially impair its value or fitness for its intended purpose. This statutory warranty applies to sales of goods and provides the foundation for contractual R&W in Thai law.
"R&W turn assumptions into enforceable commitments — what you do not warrant, you do not guarantee."

Fundamental vs General R&W

CategoryExamplesTypical Protection
Fundamental R&WTitle to shares, corporate existence, authority to enter the contractNo Cap, longer survival period
General R&WNo litigation, tax compliance, employee mattersSubject to Cap and Basket
Tax R&WAll taxes paid, no tax disputesOften separate Cap, extended survival

Under CCC Section 472, if the buyer knew of a defect at the time of sale, the seller is not liable for warranty. This principle of "buyer awareness" applies to R&W: if the buyer already knows a representation is inaccurate through due diligence, claiming breach may be more difficult. This is why disclosure schedules are critical.

Disclosure Schedules

Disclosure schedules are annexes where the warranting party lists exceptions to its R&W. For example, if the seller warrants "no pending litigation," but there is one minor case, it is listed in the disclosure schedule. Items properly disclosed are carved out from the warranty, protecting the seller from claims on known issues.

Practical Tips

For the Warranting Party (Seller)

  • Qualify R&W with "to the best of knowledge" where appropriate
  • Use materiality qualifiers to avoid claims for trivial breaches
  • Prepare thorough disclosure schedules to carve out known issues
  • Negotiate a short survival period for general R&W (12-18 months)

For the Receiving Party (Buyer)

  • Resist excessive knowledge qualifiers — the seller knows its own business
  • Require R&W to be true at signing AND at closing (Bring-Down)
  • Insist on "anti-sandbagging" provisions that preserve claims regardless of buyer's knowledge
  • Ensure Fundamental R&W survive indefinitely or for the statute of limitations period
