5 Contracts Every Business Must Have

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Does your business have all five of these essential contracts? Many entrepreneurs start their businesses without any written agreements, relying solely on verbal promises and trust. It is only when disputes arise that they discover they have no legal protection whatsoever.

Contracts exist not merely for use when disputes occur, but to prevent disputes from arising in the first place. Under Thai law, the Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) Section 354 provides that a contract is formed when the parties express matching intention through offer and acceptance. This article introduces the five minimum contractual shields every business must have.

1. Employment Agreement

An employment agreement under CCC Section 575 establishes the relationship where an employee agrees to work for an employer in exchange for wages. This contract protects both parties by defining duties, compensation, termination conditions, and intellectual property rights. Without a clear employment agreement, employers may face severance pay obligations under the Labour Protection Act B.E. 2541, Section 118, of up to 400 days' wages for employees with over 20 years of service.

Protects against: Wage disputes, unfair dismissal claims, client poaching, and IP ownership conflicts.

2. Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

An NDA protects the core information of your business — client lists, formulas, marketing strategies, and financial data. The Trade Secrets Act B.E. 2545, Section 3, defines trade secrets as commercially valuable information that is not generally known, and for which the owner has taken reasonable measures to maintain secrecy. An NDA serves as such a "reasonable measure" under the law.

Protects against: Leakage of trade secrets and competitors obtaining proprietary business information.

3. Shareholders' Agreement (SHA)

When conducting business with partners, a partnership or shareholders' agreement is essential. CCC Section 1012 provides that a partnership is formed when two or more persons agree to unite for a common undertaking. An SHA establishes critical mechanisms such as voting rights, dividend distribution, exit mechanisms, and deadlock resolution procedures to prevent business paralysis from shareholder conflicts.

Protects against: Shareholder deadlocks, management disputes, and minority shareholder oppression.

4. Lease Agreement

A property lease under CCC Section 537 is a contract whereby the lessor agrees to let the lessee use or enjoy benefits from the property for a limited period. Crucially, Section 538 requires that leases of immovable property exceeding three years must be registered; otherwise, enforcement is limited to three years. A well-drafted lease must clearly specify rent, duration, renewal rights, and premises return conditions.

Protects against: Unexpected eviction, disputes over rent payments and property maintenance obligations.

5. Service Agreement

When engaging consultants, IT providers, or contractors, a service agreement defines the scope of work, timeline, compensation, and quality standards. Under CCC Section 587, a contract for work requires the contractor to complete a specified task. A robust service agreement should include SLAs (Service Level Agreements), limitation of liability clauses, and early termination provisions.

Protects against: Substandard deliverables, service failures, and scope disputes.

Conclusion

These five contracts constitute the minimum legal shield every business must maintain, whether an SME or a large corporation. Investing in proper contracts from the outset is always more cost-effective than resolving disputes after they arise.
