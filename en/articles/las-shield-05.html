Thai Labour Law — Employer's Essential Guide

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Terminating an employee the wrong way may cost you several times more than expected. Many employers know the term "severance pay" but are unaware of the many additional rights and obligations under Thai labour law. Non-compliance can result in severance pay, damages, and advance notice compensation.

Governing Law

The Labour Protection Act B.E. 2541 (as amended by Amendment No. 9, B.E. 2568) is the primary law governing employer-employee relationships in Thailand.

Termination of Employment (Section 17)

Section 17 provides that for employment contracts without a fixed term, either party may terminate by giving written advance notice of not less than one wage payment period, effective on the next wage payment date.

Maternity Leave (Section 41 — Amendment No. 9, B.E. 2568)

Under the amended Section 41, female employees are entitled to maternity leave of up to 120 days per pregnancy (increased from 98 days). Section 41/1 introduces a new right: male employees or spouses are entitled to 15 working days of paternity/spousal leave to assist with childbirth. Section 59 requires employers to pay wages during maternity leave for up to 60 days (increased from 45 days).

Key changes in Amendment No. 9 (B.E. 2568):
  • Maternity leave increased from 98 to 120 days (Section 41)
  • New paternity/spousal leave of 15 working days (Section 41/1)
  • Paid maternity leave increased from 45 to 60 days (Section 59)

Exceptions to Severance Pay (Section 119)

Section 119 provides exceptions where severance pay is not required: dishonesty toward duties, intentional damage to the employer, serious violation of work rules after written warning (except for serious offences), abandonment of duties for 3 consecutive working days, gross negligence causing serious damage, or imprisonment by final court judgment.

Conclusion

Labour law is unavoidable for employers. Understanding rights and obligations correctly prevents disputes and builds strong relationships with employees — every business's most valuable asset.
