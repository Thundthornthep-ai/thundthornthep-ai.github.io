Franchise Law in Thailand — What to Know Before Buying

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

A franchise looks like the fast track to business success — a ready-made brand, established systems, and training. But many franchisees discover too late that the agreement is one-sided, exit is expensive, and margins are thin. Understanding the legal framework before signing is essential.

Legal Status of Franchising in Thailand

Thailand does not have a specific Franchise Act, unlike the United States, Australia, or Malaysia. Franchise agreements in Thailand are governed by multiple laws:

  • Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) — Freedom of contract (Sections 149-151) and good faith principles (Section 5)
  • Consumer Protection Act B.E. 2522 (1979) — Controls misleading advertising by franchisors
  • Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 (1997) — Courts can modify terms that are excessively one-sided
  • Trademark Act B.E. 2534 (1991) — Trademark license agreements must be registered with the Registrar

Red Flags Before Buying a Franchise

1. Read the Full Agreement, Not Just the Brochure

Brochures often project impressive revenue figures, but the actual agreement may contain harsh terms — such as early termination penalties equal to 100% of the franchise fee, or a 3-5 year post-termination non-compete clause.

2. Verify Intellectual Property Rights

Is the trademark actually registered? Under the Trademark Act B.E. 2534, a trademark license must be in writing and registered with the Registrar. If the franchisor has not registered the trademark, their right to license it may be questionable.

3. Watch for Unfair Terms

If the agreement allows the franchisor to terminate immediately without notice while requiring the franchisee to give 6 months' notice, the court may invoke Section 4 of the Unfair Contract Terms Act B.E. 2540 to adjust the terms.

Practical Tip: Before signing a franchise agreement, have a lawyer review at least three critical points: (1) termination conditions and penalties, (2) territory rights, and (3) post-termination non-compete restrictions.

Conclusion

Franchising can be an excellent business model, but it is not a guaranteed path to profit. Every clause in the franchise agreement must be understood before committing capital. Once signed, renegotiation leverage drops significantly.
