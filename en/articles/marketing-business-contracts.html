Introduction — Business Agreements in the Marketing World Why Written Contracts Are Indispensable

A Business Agreement is a contract between two or more parties that creates legally binding obligations. In the marketing industry — where relationships are built on trust, creative output, and strategic information — having a well-drafted contract is not optional; it is the foundation of every professional engagement. Written contracts help reduce disputes, maintain working relationships, and protect the rights of all parties involved.

Under Thai law, contracts are generally valid whether oral or written. However, certain types of contracts are required by law to be in writing to be enforceable, and written contracts serve as the primary form of evidence in dispute resolution. For any business relationship of commercial significance, a written agreement is always the right approach.

6 Benefits of Written Contracts

1

Legal Requirement

Required by law for certain types of contracts — failure to comply renders them unenforceable.

2

Defined Duties

Clearly define the duties and responsibilities of each party, eliminating ambiguity.

3

Admissible as evidence in court, providing the strongest form of legal protection if disputes arise.

1. Exclusive Rights vs. Non-Exclusive Rights A Fundamental Distinction in Every Marketing Contract

One of the most consequential distinctions in any marketing contract is whether the rights granted are exclusive or non-exclusive. The parties must negotiate and clearly document this distinction, as it directly affects competitive positioning, pricing, and remedies for breach.

Type Meaning Marketing Examples Risk Level
Exclusive Right The licensee holds the sole right within a defined scope — no other party may be granted the same right by the licensor during the term. Sole distributor for a territory; influencer barred from working with competing brands; exclusive brand ambassador for a product category. HIGH
Non-Exclusive Right The rights holder may grant the same rights to multiple parties simultaneously — no exclusivity guarantee is given to any single licensee. Shared logo usage license; multiple distributors in the same territory; multiple brand ambassadors simultaneously. LOW
Practical Insight

When negotiating exclusivity, always define the scope precisely: product category, geographic territory, time period, and the specific platforms or channels covered. Overly broad exclusivity provisions are a common source of disputes. Conversely, under-specified exclusivity clauses often provide weaker protection than the parties intended.

2. Influencer Contract The Most Common Marketing Agreement in the Digital Age

Influencer contracts are among the most frequently executed agreements in today's marketing environment. As influencer marketing has grown into a multi-billion baht industry in Thailand, the importance of comprehensive, well-drafted contracts has grown in parallel. An under-specified influencer agreement is one of the most common sources of brand-side losses in marketing disputes.

Every influencer contract should address the following 8 key provisions in detail:

8 Key Provisions of an Influencer Contract

1

Platform Specification

Specify every platform explicitly — Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, TikTok, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Threads, or any other platform. Do not use general language such as "social media" without listing the specific channels covered. Each platform has different reach, audience demographics, and content norms, all of which affect compensation and performance expectations.

2

Content Specifications

Define the content format (photo, video, Reel, Story, long-form), minimum post count, minimum video length (where applicable), required tone of voice, mandatory brand mentions, hashtag requirements, and whether brand approval is required before posting. Ambiguity in content specifications is the most frequent source of influencer contract disputes.

3

Contract Duration

State the contract start and end dates clearly. Include the required posting frequency (e.g., two posts per week), the deadline for each deliverable, and any post-termination obligations such as maintaining previously posted content for a defined period.

4

Compensation and Payment Terms

Specify the total fee, payment schedule (advance vs. post-delivery installments), the trigger events for each payment, and any performance-based bonuses tied to engagement metrics (views, reach, conversions). Address withholding tax obligations under Thai Revenue Code, as payments to influencers are generally subject to withholding tax.

5

IP Rights — Content Ownership

Address who owns the intellectual property in the content created: the influencer (creator) or the brand (client). Specify whether the brand may reuse, repurpose, or sublicense the content, through what channels, and for how long. If the brand requires broad reuse rights, this must typically be reflected in a higher fee.

6

Exclusivity

Specify the competing brands or product categories from which the influencer is prohibited from accepting work during the contract term. Also address whether a post-termination exclusivity period applies, and for how long. Courts will enforce reasonable exclusivity provisions, but overly broad restrictions that effectively prevent the influencer from earning a living may be challenged.

7

Confidentiality

Prohibit the influencer from disclosing confidential business information, campaign strategies, product launch plans, pricing, or any proprietary data learned through the engagement. This obligation should survive the termination of the contract for a defined period. Consider referencing or incorporating a standalone NDA for more comprehensive protection.

8

Termination

Define termination conditions for both parties — including what constitutes a material breach justifying immediate termination, the required notice period for non-cause termination, and financial consequences (penalties, refund of advances, fee reductions) tied to each termination scenario. Address whether content already published must be taken down upon termination.

Common Risk — Missing Approval Clause

One of the most commonly omitted but critical provisions is a pre-publication brand approval requirement. Without this, an influencer may publish content that does not meet brand standards, contains inaccurate product claims, or conflicts with other campaigns. Always include a mandatory approval process with defined response timelines to avoid deemed approval by silence.

3. Brand Ambassador Agreement Broader Scope, Longer Term, Higher Stakes

A brand ambassador contract covers a broader scope of work than a standard influencer agreement and typically involves a longer-term relationship. While an influencer contract is primarily focused on content creation for social media channels, a brand ambassador agreement encompasses all forms of brand representation, including advertising shoots, events, public appearances, and image obligations.

  • Advertising Shoots: TV commercials, print campaigns, digital advertising — specify the number of shooting days, location (studio or on-location), geographic rights for the resulting materials (Thailand only, regional, global), and the media channels on which the ambassador's image may be used.
  • Events and Public Appearances: Product launches, press conferences, store openings, trade expos — specify the number of required appearances per year, the minimum advance notice period required, and whether any categories of events are excluded.
  • Image and Conduct Obligations: Define requirements for maintaining a public image consistent with the brand's values, including restrictions on appearance changes (hair color, body modifications), public statements, social media behavior, and association with competing brands.
  • Duration: Brand ambassador agreements typically run for one to two years, with optional renewal provisions. Clearly address what happens to previously recorded advertising materials if the contract is not renewed.
  • Morality Clause: A morality clause is essential in any brand ambassador agreement. It provides the brand with a right to terminate the contract immediately, without liability, if the ambassador engages in behavior that materially harms the brand's reputation — such as criminal conduct, public statements contrary to brand values, or social media controversies. The morality clause should define triggering events with reasonable specificity to be enforceable.
Note on Thai Law: Under the Civil and Commercial Code of Thailand, contracts must comply with general principles of legality and good morals. Provisions that are contrary to public order or good morals are void (Section 150). Excessively punitive penalty clauses may be reduced by a court to a reasonable amount (Section 383). Both considerations are relevant when drafting morality clause remedies and liquidated damages provisions.

4. Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) The Foundation of Every Business Relationship

An NDA is a fundamental agreement that every business must have in place before sharing any sensitive information with external parties. This is especially critical in marketing, where campaign strategies, product roadmaps, pricing structures, customer data, and competitive intelligence are routinely shared with agencies, influencers, production companies, and research firms.

Under the Trade Secrets Act B.E. 2545 (2002), information qualifies for trade secret protection only if the owner takes "reasonable steps" to maintain its secrecy. An executed NDA is evidence that reasonable steps were taken — making NDA execution a prerequisite for claiming trade secret protection in Thailand.

Elements of an Effective NDA

  • Definition of Confidential Information: Define precisely what information is covered — including written, oral, electronic, and visual information. The definition should be broad enough to capture all relevant information but specific enough to be enforceable. Include examples and a catch-all provision covering all information designated as confidential at the time of disclosure.
  • Obligations of the Receiving Party: Prohibit disclosure to any third party without prior written consent. Restrict use of confidential information to the stated purpose of the engagement only. Require equivalent protective measures to those applied to the receiving party's own confidential information.
  • Duration of Obligations: Specify the period during which the obligations apply. The confidentiality obligation should survive the termination of the NDA itself for a defined number of years — typically two to five years for marketing agreements. For trade secrets, an indefinite duration is more appropriate.
  • Standard Exceptions: The four universally recognized exceptions are: information that is or becomes publicly available through no breach by the receiving party; information that the receiving party can demonstrate was already in its possession prior to disclosure; information received from a third party without restriction; and information required to be disclosed by law, court order, or regulatory mandate (subject to prompt notice to the disclosing party).
  • Remedies for Breach: Specify the remedies available including actual damages, liquidated damages, and — critically — the right to seek injunctive relief (court order prohibiting further disclosure). Expressly acknowledge that monetary damages may be inadequate for breach of confidentiality, which supports the grant of injunctive relief by courts.
Best Practice

Execute NDAs as standalone agreements before any substantive discussions begin — do not rely on confidentiality clauses embedded in later contracts. The NDA establishes the protective framework from the first moment sensitive information is shared. For recurring engagements, consider a master NDA that covers all information shared under an ongoing relationship.

5. Event Organizer Contract Scope, Budget, Timeline, and Risk Allocation

Event organizer contracts are among the most complex marketing agreements because of the number of moving variables involved: venue, vendors, staffing, timing, weather, and audience experience. A well-drafted event contract allocates risk clearly and provides enforceable mechanisms for performance management.

Provision Required Details
Event Concept Event concept and theme, format (conference, product launch, gala, roadshow), desired atmosphere and brand alignment.
Scope of Work Complete scope of services: venue sourcing and management, decoration design, stage construction, audiovisual systems, lighting, catering coordination, registration management, and post-event teardown.
Budget Total budget, line-item cost breakdown by category, process for managing scope changes and budget variations, and approval thresholds for unilateral spending decisions by the organizer.
Timeline Full project timeline from contract execution through: planning milestones, vendor booking deadlines, set-up and rehearsal dates, event day schedule, and post-event obligations (reporting, teardown, asset return).
Staffing Minimum number of staff on-site, required qualifications, uniform standards, background check requirements for security-sensitive roles.
KPIs Measurable performance indicators: confirmed attendance target, post-event satisfaction survey score, social media coverage requirements, response time SLAs for on-site issues.
Penalties Liquidated damages for delays, failure to meet KPIs, or non-compliance with specifications. Address cancellation and postponement fees, including the allocation of sunk costs already incurred.
Insurance Mandatory event liability insurance, accident insurance covering attendees, property damage coverage, and — where applicable — event cancellation insurance. The client should be named as an additional insured.

6. Market Research Agreement Defining Output, Ownership, and Confidentiality

Market research agreements govern engagements with research agencies and consultants to study consumer behavior, competitive landscapes, and market trends. Because market research outputs often contain highly sensitive strategic information, these agreements require careful attention to IP ownership and confidentiality provisions.

  • Research Objective: Specify precisely what questions the research must answer — consumer behavior patterns, competitor positioning, market size estimates, pricing sensitivity, or channel preferences. Ambiguous objectives lead to deliverables that do not meet the client's actual needs.
  • Project Duration: Define the project start and end dates, including milestone delivery dates for interim reports, data collection completion, and final analysis.
  • Compensation and Payment Schedule: Specify the total fee and an installment-based payment schedule tied to project milestones (e.g., 30% on signing, 40% on data collection completion, 30% on final report delivery).
  • Deliverable Format: Specify the required format for all deliverables — PowerPoint executive presentation, detailed PDF report, interactive dashboard, raw data files, or a combination. Failure to specify format is a common cause of deliverable disputes.
  • IP Ownership: Specify whether the research outputs (data, analysis, reports, methodologies) belong to the client upon full payment, or whether the research firm retains any rights. If the research firm may publish or reuse anonymized data for its own purposes, this must be addressed explicitly.
  • Confidentiality: Market research data — including respondent information, raw data, interim findings, and strategic conclusions — is confidential business information. The research firm and all personnel involved must be bound by confidentiality obligations extending beyond the contract term.

7. Product Testing Agreement Pre-Launch Testing with Legal Safeguards

Product testing agreements govern arrangements where testers — whether individual consumers, professionals, or influencers — trial products before public launch and provide structured feedback. These agreements are particularly critical for protecting pre-launch product information.

  • Testing Method: Define how testers must use the product — frequency of use, conditions of use, any prohibited uses, and the testing period duration.
  • Compensation: Specify whether testers receive monetary compensation, products as compensation, or a combination of both. Address tax treatment if applicable.
  • Feedback Requirements: Define the feedback format (structured questionnaire, video review, written report), frequency of feedback submissions, and the channel through which feedback must be delivered.
  • IP Ownership of Feedback: Specify that all test results, evaluations, and feedback provided by the tester become the property of the company upon delivery, with no residual rights retained by the tester.
  • Non-Compete Obligation: Prohibit testers from participating in testing programs for directly competing products during the testing period and for a defined period after its conclusion.
  • Pre-Launch Confidentiality: This is the most critical provision. Testers must be expressly prohibited from disclosing any information about the product, its features, pricing, or launch timeline until the company's official public launch announcement. Any breach must trigger immediate, specific remedies including injunctive relief.
Critical Risk — Pre-Launch Information Leakage

Product information leaks before official launch can result in significant competitive harm, compromised pricing power, and supply chain disruption. Ensure that product testing agreements include strong pre-launch confidentiality provisions, specific liquidated damages for breach, and — where the product is particularly sensitive — consider executing a separate standalone NDA in addition to the confidentiality clause in the testing agreement.

8. Other Related Commercial Contracts Additional Agreements in the Marketing Ecosystem

The marketing and business ecosystem involves a range of additional commercial agreements that businesses should be aware of:

  • TV Airtime Purchase Agreement: Governs the purchase of advertising airtime on television broadcasters, specifying the broadcast schedule, guaranteed ratings (GRP), make-good provisions for under-delivery, and exclusion of competing brands within the same time block.
  • Agency / Broker Agreement: Governs the appointment of an agent or broker for marketing and distribution purposes, including authority limits, commission structure, territorial rights, and termination consequences — particularly important given Thai law's specific provisions on agency relationships under the Civil and Commercial Code.
  • Pledge Agreement: Used as security for a contract's performance obligations, particularly in high-value engagements where performance risk is material.
  • Sales Agreement: The foundational agreement for all product transactions, specifying price, delivery terms, title transfer, payment, warranties, and dispute resolution — applicable to product launches, promotional merchandise, and campaign-related product sales.

9. Expert Recommendations 5 Principles for Every Marketing Contract

1

Always Put It in Writing

Formalize every commercial relationship in writing — even when the other party is a friend, a long-standing partner, or a family member. Oral agreements are legally valid in Thailand but extremely difficult to prove and enforce. A written contract eliminates ambiguity about what was agreed.

2

Correctly Identify the Parties

Always confirm the full legal name of every contracting party, the corporate registration number (for juristic persons), the registered address, and the name and authority of the person signing. A contract signed by an unauthorized representative may not bind the entity.

3

Set Clear, Measurable KPIs

Vague performance standards lead to disputes. Define KPIs that are specific, measurable, achievable, and time-bound. Specify the measurement methodology and who is responsible for tracking and reporting performance against each KPI.

4

Specify Termination Conditions Clearly

Both parties must understand — at the time of signing — exactly how and under what circumstances the contract may be terminated, what notice is required, and what the financial consequences of each termination scenario are. Termination provisions that are vague or silent on key scenarios are a primary source of litigation.

5

Consult a Lawyer Before Signing

For any contract involving significant value, long duration, exclusivity, or risk — consult a business lawyer before executing. The cost of legal review is almost always lower than the cost of resolving a dispute that could have been prevented by better contract drafting.
