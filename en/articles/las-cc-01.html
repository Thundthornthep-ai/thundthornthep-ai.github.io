Anatomy of a Thai Contract — 8 Core Components You Must Know

Every contract shares the same anatomy — know the structure and you can read any contract

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

Why Understand Contract Anatomy?

Many business owners receive a contract from a counterparty and read it from the first page to the last, skipping anything they do not understand and signing because they trust the other side. What is missing is a "map" of the contract. Once you understand the structure, you know which sections to scrutinize, which are standard, and which hide risk.

Under Section 354 of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC), a contract is formed when the parties express a matching offer and acceptance. In practice, however, modern business contracts are far more complex than a simple "offer + acceptance." A well-drafted contract must comprehensively allocate rights, obligations, risks, and exit mechanisms.

"A good lawyer does not draft a contract that gives one side maximum advantage — a good lawyer drafts a contract where both sides know in advance what will happen in every scenario."

The 8 Core Components

1. Recitals / Preamble

The recitals identify the contracting parties: names, addresses, corporate registration numbers, authorized signatories, and the purpose of the transaction. If the company name or authorized signatory does not match the corporate certificate, the contract may not bind the company under CCC Sections 1167 and 1168.

2. Definitions

Definitions fix the meaning of key terms used throughout the contract, such as "Business Day," "Force Majeure," or "Products." A definition that is too narrow or too broad will affect every clause that references it.

3. Operative Clauses

The core substance of the contract: scope of work, pricing, payment terms, delivery schedules, and performance standards. Operative clauses must be clear, measurable, and unambiguous.

4. Conditions Precedent (CP)

Conditions that must be satisfied before the contract becomes effective or before a party must perform. Under CCC Section 183, a condition precedent suspends the effect of a juristic act until the condition is fulfilled.

5. Representations & Warranties (R&W)

Statements by each party that certain facts are true as of the contract date — for example, that the company is duly registered, has no pending litigation, and complies with environmental laws. If an R&W turns out to be untrue, the other party may claim damages.

6. Limitation of Liability

Sets a maximum cap on liability and excludes certain types of damages such as consequential damages. Under CCC Section 373, parties cannot contractually exclude liability for fraud or gross negligence.

7. Termination

Specifies the conditions and procedures for ending the contract, including Termination for Cause and Termination for Convenience, as well as Cure Periods. CCC Sections 386 through 389 govern contractual and statutory termination rights.

8. Boilerplate

Standard clauses found at the end of nearly every contract: Governing Law, Dispute Resolution, Entire Agreement, Amendment, and Notices. Although "standard," boilerplate clauses can have significant consequences if not reviewed carefully.

Practical Tips

Read in 3 Steps

  1. Read Definitions first — understand the "language" of the contract
  2. Read Operative Clauses + Termination — understand rights, obligations, and exit routes
  3. Read Liability + R&W + Boilerplate — assess hidden risks

Caution

A contract missing one of these 8 components is not necessarily void, but it means there is a "gap" that the law or the courts will fill — and the result may not match the parties' intentions. Drafting a complete contract is the best risk prevention strategy.
