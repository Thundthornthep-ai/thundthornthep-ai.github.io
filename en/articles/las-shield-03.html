Non-Compete Clauses — Limits and Enforceability

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

A key employee resigns and joins your direct competitor the next week — what can you do? Non-compete clauses are vital tools for protecting businesses, but under Thai law, they do not always hold up. If the restrictions are too broad, courts may declare them void.

Legal Foundation

The Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) Section 150 provides that any act with an objective that is expressly prohibited by law, impossible, or contrary to public order or good morals shall be void. Non-compete clauses that excessively restrict freedom of occupation tend to be struck down under this provision.

How Thai Courts Interpret Non-Competes

Thai Supreme Court precedent establishes that non-compete restrictions must be reasonable, evaluated across three dimensions:

ElementCriteriaLikely EnforceableLikely Unenforceable
DurationMust be proportionate1-2 years5-10 years or lifetime
GeographyMust specify clear boundariesCity or province where employee workedNationwide or worldwide without justification
ScopeMust be limited to relevant activitiesSame position with direct competitorsAny position in the entire industry

The Proportionality Principle

Thai courts apply a proportionality test considering: (1) whether the employer has a legitimate interest to protect (e.g., trade secrets, client relationships); (2) whether the restriction is necessary to protect that interest; (3) whether the restriction is no broader than necessary; and (4) whether the restriction completely prevents the employee from earning a living.

Key Considerations

No Blue Pencil Doctrine

Unlike English or American courts that may sever the offending portion, Thai courts tend to void the entire clause if restrictions are too broad. The clause must be drafted correctly from the start.

Compensation for Non-Compete

Courts consider whether the employer provides special compensation for the restriction. A non-compete with compensation is more likely enforceable than one without.

Practical advice: Draft non-competes as narrowly as possible while still protecting legitimate interests. Limit duration to 2 years maximum, specify geography clearly, restrict only directly relevant work, and consider providing compensation for the restriction.

Conclusion

Non-compete clauses are useful but must be crafted carefully under Thai law. Overly broad restrictions intended to cover every scenario may result in no protection at all. A narrow but clear clause provides more real protection than a sweeping one.
