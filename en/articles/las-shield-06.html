Force Majeure — When Can You Invoke It?

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Floods, earthquakes, pandemics, wars — these events can make contractual performance impossible. But what does the law actually say? Force Majeure is a legal principle every business owner must understand, as it can serve as either a shield or a trap, depending on whether it is invoked correctly.

Definition Under Thai Law

The Civil and Commercial Code (CCC) Section 8 defines "force majeure" as any event, the happening or ill effects of which could not be prevented even though the person who encounters or is about to encounter such event has exercised such care as might be expected from a person in that position and in those circumstances.

Legal Consequences

Impossibility of Performance (Section 219)

CCC Section 219 provides that if performance becomes impossible due to a circumstance for which the debtor is not responsible, the debtor is released from the obligation. In other words, if force majeure makes contract performance completely impossible, the obligor bears no liability.

Bilateral Contracts (Section 372)

CCC Section 372 provides that in bilateral contracts, if performance becomes impossible due to a cause not attributable to the debtor, the debtor loses the right to demand counter-performance. Both parties are released from their obligations.

COVID-19 and Force Majeure in Thailand

  • May invoke: When government orders (e.g., Emergency Decree on Public Administration in Emergency Situations B.E. 2548) directly shut down the business, making performance impossible
  • Cannot invoke: When circumstances merely make performance more difficult or expensive but still possible — "harder" is not "impossible"

Practical advice: Every business contract should include a force majeure clause that specifies: (1) qualifying events, (2) notification procedure, (3) consequences (suspension or termination), and (4) maximum suspension period before either party may terminate.

Conclusion

Force majeure is not a catch-all excuse. It must meet all legal requirements. The most important step is drafting a clear force majeure clause from the outset to avoid arguments when an event actually occurs.
