Force Majeure vs Hardship

"Cannot perform" vs "Can perform but at great cost" — the law treats them very differently

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

Force Majeure Under Thai Law

Force majeure is one of the most critical legal concepts in an era of continuous unforeseen events, from pandemics and trade wars to natural disasters.

CCC Section 8: "Force majeure denotes any event the happening or pernicious result of which could not be prevented even though a person against whom it happened or was about to happen had exercised such care as might be expected from a person in his position and circumstances."

Under CCC Section 219, paragraph 1, when performance becomes impossible through no fault of the debtor, the debtor is discharged from the obligation. This means that if a genuine force majeure event renders performance impossible, the affected party bears no liability.

"Force majeure excuses performance entirely — hardship merely demands renegotiation. Two paths, two very different outcomes."

Force Majeure vs Hardship

CriterionForce MajeureHardship
EssencePerformance is impossiblePerformance is still possible but fundamentally more burdensome
Legal EffectDischarge from obligation / terminationRenegotiation of contract terms
Thai LawExpressly provided (Sections 8, 219)No specific provision — must be written into the contract
ExampleFactory destroyed by floodingRaw material costs increase 300% but production is still feasible

Under UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts, Article 6.2.2 defines hardship as occurring when supervening events fundamentally alter the equilibrium of the contract, whether through increased cost of performance or decreased value of what is received.

Drafting a Force Majeure Clause

Essential Elements

  1. Definition — list qualifying events: natural disasters, war, pandemics, government orders (use a non-exhaustive list)
  2. Notice Obligation — require the affected party to notify the other within a specified period (e.g., 7 days)
  3. Mitigation Duty — the party claiming force majeure must take reasonable steps to reduce the impact
  4. Consequences — suspension of obligations during the event, or termination if force majeure continues beyond a specified period

Caution

  • Thai law has no hardship doctrine — if you need protection, you must draft a separate Hardship Clause
  • Avoid exhaustive (closed) force majeure lists — events not listed will not qualify
  • Post-2020 pandemics may no longer qualify as force majeure because they are now "foreseeable"
