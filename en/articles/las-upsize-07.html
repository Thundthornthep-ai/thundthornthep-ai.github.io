Fundraising — Legal Framework for Startups

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Startups seeking growth capital face a critical question — how to raise funds without losing control of the company? The answer lies in understanding the legal structure of each fundraising instrument.

Equity vs Debt Financing

FactorEquity (Sell Shares)Debt (Borrow)
What you receiveInvestment capital in exchange for ownershipLoan to be repaid with interest
DilutionYes — founders' ownership decreasesNo — ownership unaffected
ObligationsNo repayment, but share profits and controlMust repay on schedule, may require collateral
RiskInvestors gain voting rights and board controlDefault may trigger collateral enforcement

Securities Offering Framework

The Securities and Exchange Act B.E. 2535 (1992) governs securities offerings in Thailand with two main categories:

1. Private Placement (PP)

Offering securities to no more than 50 investors within 12 months, or to institutional/high-net-worth investors. No filing with the SEC is required. This is the most common method for Seed and Series A rounds.

2. Public Offering (PO)

Requires filing a registration statement and prospectus with the SEC. Higher costs and regulatory burden. Suitable for companies ready for public listing.

Common Startup Fundraising Instruments

1. Convertible Note

A debt instrument that converts into equity upon a trigger event, typically the next funding round. Features a Discount Rate (price discount for early investors) and a Valuation Cap (maximum valuation for conversion).

2. SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

Developed by Y Combinator. Not debt — no interest, no maturity date. Automatically converts to equity upon the next priced round. Must be adapted to comply with the Thai Civil and Commercial Code when used in Thailand.

3. Preferred Shares

Shares with special rights: priority dividends, liquidation preference, conversion rights, and anti-dilution protection. Under CCC Section 1108(5), a company may issue preferred shares as specified in its articles of association.

Practical Tip: Before accepting investment, understand what you are giving up — ownership percentage, decision-making power, board seats, and veto rights. Negotiate the term sheet carefully and have a lawyer review every document.

Conclusion

Fundraising is a transformative moment for any startup, but it must be done lawfully and prudently. Choosing the right instrument and negotiating fair terms from the start will prevent significant problems down the road.
