From Solo Business to Company — When to Incorporate

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

Your business started as a one-person operation. Revenue is growing, clients are multiplying, but you are still operating as an individual. The critical question: when should you incorporate? The answer depends not just on revenue, but on legal risk, tax efficiency, and business opportunity.

Three Business Structures Under Thai Law

1. Sole Proprietorship

No registration as a juristic person required. The owner bears unlimited personal liability for all business debts. There is no legal separation between personal and business assets. Creditors can enforce against the owner's personal property without limitation.

2. Partnership

Under Section 1012 of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC), a partnership is a contract whereby two or more persons agree to unite for a common undertaking with a view to sharing the profits. There are two forms: an ordinary partnership (all partners bear unlimited liability) and a limited partnership (some partners have limited liability) under CCC Section 1077.

3. Limited Company

Under CCC Section 1096, a limited company is one formed by dividing capital into equal shares, where shareholders' liability is limited to the amount remaining unpaid on their shares. This is the most popular structure because it clearly separates personal assets from business assets.

Checklist: Signs You Should Incorporate

  • Annual revenue exceeding THB 1.8 million (VAT registration threshold under the Revenue Code)
  • Need to separate personal assets from business risk
  • Major clients or partners require contracts with a juristic person
  • Planning to raise investment or obtain bank loans
  • More than 5 employees — increasing labor management costs
  • Want SME tax benefits under the Small and Medium Enterprises Promotion Act B.E. 2543
  • Business faces litigation risk — need to limit liability
Practical Tip: You do not need to wait until your business is large to incorporate. If two or three of the above indicators apply, it is time to plan your business structure with a qualified lawyer.

Conclusion

Transitioning from sole proprietorship to a limited company is a pivotal step. It is not merely about taxes — it is about limiting risk, building credibility, and opening doors to new capital and growth opportunities.
