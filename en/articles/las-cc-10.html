The First Draft Advantage

In contract negotiations, the party holding the pen wields more power than you think

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

What Is the First Draft Advantage?

In every business transaction, there is a question that must be answered before negotiations begin: "Who will draft the contract?" The answer has an outsized impact on bargaining power, because the party that produces the first draft effectively "sets the playing field."

The First Draft Advantage is the concept that the drafting party controls the structure, language, definitions, and risk allocation of the contract. The other side is placed in a "reactive" rather than "proactive" position. Anything not marked up stays in the contract.

"The drafter sets the rules. The reviewer merely adjusts them — but the fundamental framework rarely changes."

5 Points of the Drafter's Advantage

1. Structure Control

The drafter determines how many clauses the contract has, their sequence, and which issues are grouped together. A well-structured draft makes the drafter's favorable terms appear "standard" while forcing the other side to "add" its protections, making them look like additional demands.

2. Definitions Control

Definitions are the heart of the contract. The drafter chooses whether each term is defined broadly or narrowly. For example, a broadly defined "Material Adverse Change" favors buyers, while a narrow definition with carve-outs favors sellers.

3. Risk Allocation

The drafter sets the "starting point" for risk allocation: what the liability cap is, what indemnity covers, where the basket sits. The counterparty must negotiate "down" from the drafter's anchor, which is harder than setting terms from scratch.

4. Boilerplate Control

The clauses at the back of the contract that are often overlooked but have high impact: Governing Law, Dispute Resolution, Assignment, Waiver. Drafters typically insert boilerplate favorable to their side, and reviewers often fail to scrutinize these provisions.

5. The Anchoring Effect

In negotiation psychology, the first number or term proposed becomes the "anchor" around which all subsequent discussions revolve. Negotiations gravitate toward the anchor, not toward a theoretically fair midpoint.

Counter-Strategies for the Reviewing Party

How to Counteract the First Draft Advantage

  1. Review Definitions first — definitions determine everything; do not start reviewing from Clause 1
  2. Consider a Counter-Draft — if the contract is excessively one-sided, draft a fresh version
  3. Review every Boilerplate clause — do not skip them because they appear "standard"
  4. Prepare an Issue List — prioritize issues: Must Have / Nice to Have / Acceptable
  5. Insist on adequate review time — never sign under time pressure

Caution: The Mark-up Process

Marking up a contract (editing with Track Changes) is an art. You must know which clauses to change, which to leave, and how to propose alternatives. Too many mark-ups may signal that you "refuse everything," while too few may mean you have accepted hidden risks. Strike a balance and focus your mark-ups on the provisions that truly matter.
