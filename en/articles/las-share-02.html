Memorandum of Association — Your Company's DNA

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

If a company were a person, the Memorandum of Association would be its DNA — defining everything from its name and address to the full scope of activities it can legally pursue.

What Is a Memorandum of Association?

The Memorandum of Association (MOA) is the founding document of a Thai limited company, required to be registered with the Registrar under Section 1098 of the Civil and Commercial Code (CCC). It is the very first step before the statutory meeting and company registration.

Mandatory Particulars Under Section 1098

Section 1098 of the CCC requires the MOA to contain:

  • (1) Company name — must always end with the word "Limited" (Section 1096)
  • (2) Registered office — the province where the head office is located
  • (3) Objectives — the business activities the company will undertake
  • (4) Registered capital — the amount of capital divided into shares, with par value
  • (5) Names, addresses, occupations, and shareholdings of the promoters — at least 3 persons (Section 1097)

Company Objectives — Too Broad vs. Too Narrow

When Objectives Are Too Narrow

If the objectives state only "selling food," expanding into catering or e-commerce would require amending the MOA first. This demands a special resolution of the shareholders' meeting (Section 1145) and registration of the amendment with the Registrar.

When Objectives Are Broad

In practice, the Department of Business Development (DBD) provides standard objective templates covering multiple activities. Most entrepreneurs use reasonably broad objectives. However, businesses requiring special licenses — such as tourism, hotels, or financial institutions — must specify objectives matching the license category.

Key Point: Activities conducted outside the stated objectives are not automatically void under civil law, but may create complications with regulatory registrations and license applications.

When Must the MOA Be Amended?

  • Change of company name — requires MOA amendment and registration
  • Relocation to another province — requires amendment of the office location
  • Change of objectives — adding or modifying business activities
  • Capital increase — requires amendment of capital particulars (Section 1220)
  • Capital decrease — requires amendment of capital particulars (Section 1224)

Amendment Procedure

Amending the MOA requires a special resolution — not less than three-fourths of the total votes of shareholders present and entitled to vote at the meeting (CCC Section 1145). The amendment must then be registered with the Registrar within 14 days (Section 1023).

Conclusion

The Memorandum of Association is a document many overlook after company registration. In reality, it defines the boundaries of what your company can lawfully do. Regularly reviewing and updating the objectives ensures your company faces no legal obstacles when the business needs to grow.
