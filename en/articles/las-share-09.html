Joint Liability vs. Several Liability in Thai Law

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

When there are multiple debtors, can the creditor demand full payment from any one of them? The answer depends on whether the obligation is "joint" or "several" — and the difference has enormous consequences.

Joint Liability (Solidary Obligation)

Under Section 291 of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code (CCC), where several debtors are bound to perform the same obligation as joint debtors, the creditor may demand full performance from any one debtor, from several, or from all of them simultaneously, until the obligation is fully satisfied.

Several Liability

Under several liability, each debtor is liable only for their individual share. The creditor cannot demand payment from one debtor beyond that debtor's portion. Unless otherwise agreed or provided by law, each debtor bears an equal share.

Comparison

Right of Recourse Among Joint Debtors

Under CCC Section 296, a joint debtor who has paid the entire obligation has a right of recourse against the other joint debtors for equal shares, unless otherwise agreed.

Practical Applications

1. Guarantee Contracts

A guarantor is not a joint debtor by law. Under CCC Section 681/1, any contractual term making a guarantor liable as a joint debtor is void. The guarantor therefore retains the right to require the creditor to enforce against the principal debtor first.

2. Company Directors

Directors who cause damage to the company through their actions are jointly and severally liable to the company (CCC Section 1169).

3. Joint Tortfeasors

Under CCC Section 432, persons who jointly commit a wrongful act are jointly liable for damages as solidary debtors.

Advice for Creditors: If you want multiple debtors to be jointly liable, the contract must expressly state that "all debtors shall be jointly and severally liable." Without such a clause, the law presumes several liability.

Conclusion

Joint liability gives creditors stronger protection because they can claim the full amount from any debtor. Several liability limits claims to each debtor's proportional share. Business owners must understand this distinction — both as creditors seeking protection and as debtors knowing their exposure.
