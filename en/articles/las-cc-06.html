Termination Clauses Done Right

Termination is not just "saying goodbye" — it requires knowing the conditions, procedures, and legal consequences

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | 3 April 2026 | Legal Advance Solution Co., Ltd.

Why Termination Clauses Matter

Termination clauses are the most overlooked provisions in business contracts. At the time of signing, all parties focus on "starting" rather than "ending." Yet in practice, a significant number of business contracts end before their natural expiry, whether due to breach, a shift in strategy, or changing market conditions.

Under CCC Section 386, the right to terminate arises when the contract grants such a right or when the law provides for it. Without clear termination provisions, the parties must rely on statutory rules that may not align with their intentions.

"A good contract is not one that never ends — it is one where both sides know in advance how it will end."

Termination for Cause vs Convenience

Termination for Cause

The right to terminate when the other party commits a material breach. Under CCC Section 387, when a party fails to perform, the other party may set a reasonable period and demand performance. If performance is not rendered within that period, the demanding party may terminate. Under CCC Section 389, if performance becomes impossible due to the debtor's fault, the creditor may terminate immediately without notice.

Termination for Convenience

The right to terminate without cause, simply by giving advance notice. This right is not expressly provided for in the CCC but is permitted under the principle of freedom of contract as recognized by CCC Section 386.

For CauseMaterial breach + notice to cureBreaching party may owe damages
For ConvenienceAdvance notice per contract termsMay require a termination fee

Cure Period & Notice Period

Cure Period: The window given to the breaching party to remedy the defect before termination takes effect. CCC Section 387 requires "a reasonable period." In practice, business contracts typically specify 15-30 days.
Notice Period: The advance notice required before termination becomes effective. Standard business contracts specify 30-90 days. Higher-value or investment-heavy contracts warrant longer notice periods.

Drafting Best Practices

  • Define "Material Breach" clearly — avoid overly broad language
  • Set Cure Periods appropriate to the type of breach
  • Specify post-termination consequences: refunds, return of property, and survival clauses
  • Include Immediate Termination rights for severe events: insolvency, fraud, or license revocation

Caution

Under CCC Section 388, if performance is not rendered within the time fixed by the contract and time is of the essence, the other party may terminate immediately without notice. Service providers must pay special attention to delivery deadlines in time-sensitive contracts.
