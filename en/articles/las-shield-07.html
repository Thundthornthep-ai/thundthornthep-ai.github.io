Warranty vs. Guarantee — What's the Difference?

Thundthornthep Yamoutai, Ph.D. | LAS Legal | 3 April 2026

"Warranty" and "guarantee" sound similar, but in law they are entirely different concepts. Many business owners use these terms interchangeably without realizing the legal consequences differ significantly. Understanding the distinction helps you choose the right legal tool for the right situation.

Warranty — Product/Service Quality Assurance

CCC Section 472 provides that where property sold is defective in a manner that diminishes either its value or its fitness for ordinary use or for the use contemplated by the contract, the seller is liable, whether or not the seller knew of the defect. A warranty is the seller's or service provider's direct assurance of quality.

Guarantee — Suretyship for Another's Debt

CCC Section 680 provides that a guarantee is a contract whereby a third person, called the guarantor, binds themselves to a creditor to perform an obligation in case the debtor fails to perform. A guarantee is a three-party arrangement where an outsider promises to pay if the debtor cannot.

For Warranty: CCC Section 473 provides that the seller is not liable if the buyer knew of the defect at the time of sale or should have known had they exercised reasonable care. Buyers must inspect goods carefully before acceptance.
For Guarantee: CCC Section 688 grants the guarantor the "right of excussion" — the right to require the creditor to pursue the debtor first before demanding payment from the guarantor, unless this right has been waived in the contract.

Conclusion

A warranty assures the quality of what is sold or delivered, while a guarantee assures that someone else's debt will be paid. Both are important risk management tools, but they apply in different situations. Understanding the distinction ensures you deploy the right legal protection for your business.
